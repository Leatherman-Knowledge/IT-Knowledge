# Net Neutral AI Usage

## The Case

As Leatherman increases its reliance on AI tooling across the organization, we should proactively commit to being net neutral on the environmental impact of that usage. AI inference and training carry real energy and carbon costs, and getting ahead of this positions Leatherman as a responsible adopter rather than a passive consumer. It's also just the right thing to do — we make tools that last generations, and that ethos should extend to how we power the technology behind them.

The goal is straightforward: measure what we use, then offset it.

---

## Measuring Our Footprint

Before we can offset anything, we need visibility into what we're actually consuming.

### Replit Dashboard — Environmental Impact Data

Replit runs on Google Cloud infrastructure, which means Google's carbon reporting and energy usage data should be available under the hood. It's worth opening a conversation with Replit to ask whether environmental impact metrics (energy consumption, carbon intensity per compute hour) can be surfaced directly on our team dashboard. Google Cloud already provides these figures through their Carbon Footprint tool, so the data likely exists — it's a matter of whether Replit exposes it.

**Open question:** Even if Replit surfaces infrastructure-level energy data, it may not capture the full picture of token-level usage if inference is routed through third-party LLM providers. We'd need to confirm whether our token consumption maps cleanly to the compute metrics Replit reports, or if there's a gap.

### Token Tracking Middleware

For LLM usage specifically, we could implement a lightweight middleware layer that logs every API call — model used, token count (input and output), estimated compute cost — and aggregates it over time. Python libraries like `openai` and `anthropic` already return token counts in their responses, so the instrumentation is straightforward.

This gives us a running ledger of our AI consumption that's independent of any single platform's reporting. It also lets us slice usage by team, project, or use case, which is useful for understanding where the spend (both financial and environmental) is concentrated.

## Offsetting the Impact

### Leatherman's Existing Solar

Leatherman already has significant solar capacity installed on-site. Depending on how much energy our solar generates versus how much our AI usage consumes, we may already be in a position to claim a partial or full offset simply by pointing to existing renewable generation.

This needs some math: estimate the annual kWh consumed by our AI workloads (using the measurement data above), compare it against the annual kWh generated by our solar installations, and determine whether the solar surplus covers the AI footprint. If it does, this is the simplest and most credible offset story — no additional spend required, just documentation.

### Carbon Credits and Local Environmental Investment

If solar alone doesn't fully cover it, or if we want to go beyond net neutral, we can purchase carbon credits (sometimes called Renewable Energy Certificates or carbon offsets, depending on the mechanism). These are tradable instruments that represent verified emissions reductions or renewable energy generation.

More compelling than generic credits would be investing in local environmental projects — reforestation, watershed restoration, renewable energy installations in the Portland area. This aligns better with Leatherman's brand identity and community roots, and it's a more tangible story than "we bought credits on an exchange."

## Marketing and Brand Value

This isn't just an internal feel-good initiative — there's a real brand story here. Leatherman's entire identity is built on durability, longevity, and not being wasteful. A public commitment to net neutral AI usage reinforces that narrative in a space where most companies are staying quiet about the environmental cost of their AI adoption.

Being early matters. Right now, very few manufacturers are talking about responsible AI usage from an environmental angle. That gives Leatherman a first-mover advantage in the conversation — press coverage, social media traction, and differentiation from competitors who are either ignoring the topic or haven't thought about it yet. "The company that builds tools to last a lifetime is also building AI practices that don't cost the planet" practically writes itself.

There's also a customer alignment play. Leatherman's audience skews toward outdoor enthusiasts, tradespeople, and people who value sustainability through product longevity. These customers care about environmental responsibility — not in a performative way, but in a "walk the talk" way. Showing that we measure and offset our AI footprint, especially if we can tie it to local Oregon projects or our own solar infrastructure, speaks directly to that audience.

Internally, it's also a recruiting and retention tool. Engineers and tech-minded employees increasingly want to work for companies that take this stuff seriously. A visible commitment to responsible AI usage signals that Leatherman thinks critically about the tools it adopts, not just whether they're useful but whether they're sustainable.

## Suggested Next Steps

1. **Reach out to Replit** about environmental impact data availability on our dashboard. Understand what they can surface and whether it captures LLM token usage or just raw compute.
2. **Prototype token tracking middleware** — even a simple Python script that wraps our LLM calls and logs usage to a database. Get a baseline of our monthly consumption.
3. **Quantify solar output vs. AI consumption** — work with facilities to get actual kWh generation numbers and compare against estimated AI energy usage.
4. **Research offset options** — carbon credits, RECs, local environmental investment programs in Oregon. Understand pricing and verification standards.
5. **Draft a proposal** — once we have the data, put together a one-pager for leadership that shows the footprint, the offset plan, and the cost (which may be zero if solar covers it).
